## Why landing pages are hard to get right

Every AI company landing page looks the same. Purple gradient. Floating product screenshot. "Powered by AI" in bold. Three feature cards. A CTA button.

The problem isn't the individual elements — it's that they've been copied so many times they've stopped meaning anything. A visitor processes the whole page in about four seconds and files it under "another AI startup."

When I started building the landing page for Scam AI, I knew I wanted something different. An AI security company that detects deepfakes, scam calls, and fraud should feel like it has teeth. Not a generic SaaS aesthetic.

What I didn't know was how many iterations it would take to get there.

## The early versions

The first version was functional and forgettable. Standard Next.js layout, Inter font, hero section with a tagline, three feature cards. I shipped it quickly to have something up.

The second version added dark mode and started experimenting with the visual identity. Black backgrounds, high-contrast typography, a navigation that felt more like a security product than a SaaS dashboard.

This is when I started understanding the real design problem: **Scam AI serves different audiences simultaneously** — enterprises buying API access, individuals worried about scam calls, and researchers interested in the underlying detection models. A single hero can't speak to all three. You have to make a choice about who you're talking to first, and trust that the rest can find their section.

## The Bento phase

One redesign cycle brought in a Bento grid — the card-based layout that became popular after Apple's product pages started using it. Dense, modular, visually interesting.

It worked for a while. The grid communicated "we do a lot of things" efficiently, and the visual hierarchy made it scannable. But it created a different problem: with 8 product verticals (deepfakes, voice clones, scam text, AI-generated images, KYC verification...), a Bento grid starts to feel like a menu rather than a pitch.

Good landing pages answer one question before the visitor asks it: *why should I care?* A menu answers a different question: *what do you sell?* Those are not the same.

## Learning to lead with the problem

The clearest version of the page came from committing to a problem-first narrative.

Instead of opening with the product, open with what happens when the product doesn't exist: a grandmother wires money to a voice clone of her grandson. A hiring manager interviews a deepfake candidate. A financial firm processes synthetic identity documents at scale.

These are visceral. They don't require the visitor to already understand what "AI-generated media detection" means. And they set up the product as the answer to something real, not just another capability to evaluate.

The hero became: show the threat, then show the shield.

## The newsletter CMS

One of the more unexpected additions was a full newsletter system built into the landing site. Scam AI publishes regular content about fraud trends, new detection techniques, and AI security research. The newsletter needed to live somewhere branded, not just in an email client.

I built an admin CMS — editor, slug-based URLs, article thumbnails, RSS feeds from external news sources — and a public reading experience that felt like a proper publication rather than a blog afterthought.

This was a different kind of challenge from the landing page itself. The landing page is about impression and conversion. The newsletter is about retention and trust. They needed different layouts, different typographic systems, and different notions of what "good" looks like.

## The hero video problem

For a while, the hero featured a dashboard demo video. Auto-playing, looping, starting at 4 seconds to skip the boring parts.

In theory: show the product in action. In practice: the video competed with the headline for attention, took significantly longer to load, and looked worse on slower connections. It also meant the hero's message was contingent on the video working — on mobile, it often didn't.

Removed it. A strong headline and a well-chosen static visual outperform a generic product demo video almost every time.

## What I'd do differently

**Commit to one audience earlier.** The page oscillated between enterprise and consumer for too long. Every time we tried to speak to both at once, we diluted the message for both. Pick the primary user and design for them; everyone else can find their section.

**Treat the headline as the hardest design problem.** I spent more time on nav animations and color tokens than on the actual words in the hero. That's backwards. The headline is the only element every visitor definitely reads. Everything else is optional.

**Version control the copy.** Design systems track component changes carefully. Marketing copy changes are tracked nowhere. I'd lost context on why certain phrasings existed, what they were testing against, and whether the change had been validated.
