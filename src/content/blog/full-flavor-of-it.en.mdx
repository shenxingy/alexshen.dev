It was 1:30 AM. The kind of late that's only possible when you're either procrastinating or actually in the middle of something that matters.

Dennis had been real with me. And I was being real back.

> *"坚信现阶段还是经历最重要，学知识、见世面、创造价值是我的热爱"*
>
> *— Alex, 1:32 AM*

Then, a few minutes later:

> *"但猛猛干，乘风破浪才更有意思哈哈哈"*
>
> *"人生需要来点酸甜苦辣咸"*
>
> *"风平浪静一帆风顺，就像顿顿清汤寡水"*

This was not a planned manifesto. It was a late-night text between two people who genuinely like each other and aren't afraid to say the hard thing out loud.

But it had more truth in it than most things I write deliberately.

---

## The Flat Water Problem

There's a particular kind of life that's easy to optimize for. Good sleep schedule. Stable income. A apartment that's close to everything and not too close to anything. A relationship that's comfortable. A diet that's manageable.

You can run that life on autopilot. Nothing wrong with it — on paper, it's a life many people would trade for.

But there's a problem with flat water: it doesn't taste like anything.

You wake up, you execute, you sleep. The days pass at a steady pace. Nothing bad happens. Nothing particularly interesting happens either. You're technically alive, but you're not *tasting* it.

I grew up eating Sichuan food. My palate was trained on the interplay of 麻 (numbing peppercorn) and 辣 (chili fire), the slow build of 酸 (vinegar brightness), the depth of 咸 (salt). A meal without at least a few of those elements felt like it was missing something. Not bad, just... incomplete.

Life works the same way.

---

## What "乘风破浪" Actually Means

The phrase gets used casually in Chinese — riding the waves, pushing through challenges. But watch what it implies:

**Wind.** You can't control when it comes. You can't always predict its direction. It can come as a headwind that slows you down or a tailwind that accelerates you faster than you expected. Either way, you don't get to choose whether there's wind. You only get to choose whether you're facing into it or running from it.

**Waves.** Things will go wrong. Not hypothetically — actually, concretely, in ways that cost you time or money or sleep. The question isn't whether you'll hit rough water. The question is what your boat looks like when you do.

**Riding.** Not conquering. Not dominating. You're not crushing the waves — you're moving *with* them while staying upright. The goal is momentum, not perfection.

Dennis said it better than I could in Chinese. 乘风破浪 — get out there and find out what you're made of when the conditions aren't ideal.

---

## The Experience Thesis

Here's the thing I believe at 1:30 AM that I'm less sure about by morning:

**Right now, at this stage, experience is worth more than knowledge.**

Not that knowledge doesn't matter. It does. But the knowledge that compounds fastest is the kind you earn by doing — by shipping something real, watching people use it, feeling where the design breaks, iterating under actual pressure. That kind of knowledge embeds itself in your judgment in a way that book learning doesn't.

学知识、见世面、创造价值. Learn, see the world, create value. That's the sequence I believe in. Not learn-learn-learn until you're "ready." Learn a little, go build something, see what happens, learn from what you hit.

The traction comes from doing. The money, when it comes, is just the market telling you that what you made was worth something to somebody.

---

## On Not Sleeping

The text ended with me saying I was going to sleep. Dennis said we should both get some rest. And then I sent one more message — the one about flat water.

I meant it.

There's a version of my life where I coast. Where I take the comfortable path, the one that doesn't ask too much of me on any given Tuesday. It would be fine. Probably better for my sleep schedule.

But I'd rather wake up at 1:30 AM arguing with a friend about why the hard thing is the right thing. I'd rather taste all five flavors — the sour setback, the sweet win, the bitter failure, the spicy ambiguity, the salt of hard work — than cruise through on the safe lane.

> *人生需要来点酸甜苦辣咸.*
>
> Life needs some sweet, sour, bitter, spicy, salty.

That's not a call to suffering. It's a call to *taste*. To be present in something that matters enough that it can actually hurt when it goes wrong and actually sing when it goes right.

Dennis is right. We got this.

Tomorrow, we push.
