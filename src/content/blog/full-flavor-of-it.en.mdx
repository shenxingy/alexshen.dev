H1B results came out today. Didn't get picked.

I found out and went right back to building.

That's not me being stoic about it. It's just the accurate description of what happened. There's work to do, the work is interesting, and one lottery doesn't change the direction. Tomorrow there's more to build, more to fix, more to figure out.

But tonight, late, I was sitting with it — the full day, the result, the running list of things that didn't go smoothly and the ones that did — and a thought landed that felt worth writing down.

---

## A Day in the Life

Here's what today actually looked like, in no particular order:

Fixing bugs and refining workflows. Training a model and watching it not converge in the way you expected. Tinkering with a performance number that needs to be better. Writing a scraper, debugging it, rewriting it. Researching a mechanism — how something works, why it works that way, whether it needs to. Talking to people, which is its own entire skill and not the easy one.

The things I thought would be hard were hard. The things I thought would be easy turned out to have depth I didn't anticipate. The interactions with people — the misalignment of expectations, the negotiation of priorities, the reading of what someone actually means versus what they're saying — that part is its own universe. Harder, in its own way, than any of the technical parts.

And all of it was interesting in a way that comfortable work isn't.

---

## On Not Getting Picked

I want to be careful here, because I don't want to write a "hardship is a gift" post. It isn't. Not getting picked for something you wanted is just disappointing. It costs time. It introduces uncertainty. It means re-examining plans you'd already started building.

That's just the bitter part. It doesn't need to be reframed as something positive.

But here's what is true: I'd rather be in this than in a version of my life where I never put myself in a position to be disappointed. Where I never built anything that could fail, never tried anything that could not work out. That's its own kind of loss — quieter, less dramatic, but still a life not fully tasted.

---

## The Flat Water You Can't Criticize

There's a Chinese saying: 风平浪静 — flat water, calm wind. The image is peaceful. Peaceful in a way that has its own genuine beauty.

I don't want to贬低 that. There's a version of life that runs smoothly — steady work, healthy routines, people you care about, sleep that comes easy. That is genuinely good. It's not a consolation prize. It's not a failure mode. It's just a mode.

The problem isn't flat water. The problem is *only* flat water.

Water is fine. Water is necessary. But if that's all you ever drink, eventually you're not getting the things that make food actually taste like something. Salt, acid, sweetness, bitterness, umami — you need all of them. Cooking needs 五味调和: the balanced harmony of flavors. Not one dominant taste. All of them, in proportion.

You can't build that from water alone.

---

## Riding the Waves

So you go back to building. You fix the bug. You retrain the model. You have the hard conversation. You ship the feature that might work, and you watch to see if it does.

乘风破浪 — ride the wind, break through the waves. That's not about being reckless or refusing to rest. It's about showing up for the parts of your life that are genuinely hard and choosing not to look away from them.

The waves come regardless. The question is whether you jump in the boat or stand on the shore.

> *人生需要来点酸甜苦辣咸.*
>
> Life needs some sweet, sour, bitter, spicy, salty.

Tomorrow, we push.
